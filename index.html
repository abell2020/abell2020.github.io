Hacking a muse EEG headset using ML to play breakout!
I hacked a muse EEG meditation headset so I could use ML to recognize patterns in raw EEG data. After hours of trying to directly connect the headband to my computer, I eventually found an app that could reliably connect to the headset and send data over OSC, and then I hacked a c# OSC receiver program on my computer to write to a file that a python program could then pull the live data from. Once I got the live data into python, I just had to record the raw EEG data while performing various actions. With only around 15 minutes worth of training data (broken up to produce around 15,000 training samples, each with 120 previous values for each of the 4 eeg channels), and a relatively simple multilayer perceptron (480 inputs, 128, 128, 3 output), I had 99% accuracy detecting left clenched, right clenched, and unclenched jaw states, and 75% accuracy detecting thinking (and looking) up, down, or straight. In the video, I’m clenching my left or right jaw to control a game of Atari breakout.

Turning a Bop it extreme into a controller
Inspired by people playing video games with baguettes and pineapples online, I thought it would be ridiculously funny to play video games using a bop it. So, over Christmas break of 2018, I put the guts of an Xbox 360 controller inside a bop it! I soldered all of the buttons inside the bop it to the buttons on the Xbox controller, and mounted joysticks to the bop it. It was extremely difficult, but I turned one of the big round bop it buttons into a joystick. I tried to preserve the bop it functionality, but sadly I had to remove the bop it circuit boards to make everything fit inside. I drove my FTC and FRC robots around with it for fun, and of course, I played video games with it. It has just enough buttons to play overwatch, and to shoot you spin the wheel :p The video is a clip from a Youtube video I made about the bop it. The full video is here: https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=Z8bw5sZtrDY&t=32s

Playing Minecraft without a mouse using my hands!
I used a Leap motion hand sensor and processing to recognize hand gestures and hand position. I wrote a program that controls all the mouse functions in Minecraft, so that instead of using a mouse with your right hand you can actually do the actions. You punch to hit things, use an open hand to place things, use two fingers to look around, and one finger swiping changes item slots. As you can see at the end, you can also pull your ring finger in and then turn your hand to cast spells. My processing program types commands into the Minecraft chat really fast to communicate with the Minecraft mod I wrote for the spellcasting. So far you can teleport, and shoot fireballs and lightning!

Vocabulary.com chrome extension bot
This is a chrome extension I wrote that automatically does Vocabulary.com questions. I was poking around the site's HTML, and noticed that some of the questions had hard coded in answers, so I couldn't help myself. At the time, I knew nothing about chrome extensions or JavaScript, so I spent the next three months learning and working on this project. For the multiple choice questions, the code actually scrapes Vocabulary.com's definition page for a word, and then determines if any of the answers are found in the definition page. The fill in the blank questions have the answers in the HTML, so they're easy. The program gets the right answer about 80% of the time, which I'm really happy with. It just automates all of the clicks, and it can actually click behind the timeout Vocabulary.com added to try to stop you from going too fast :)

Science Olympiad Hovercraft 2018
My partner and I went through so many iterations on this hovercraft. Getting the skirt just right and orienting the main downward facing fan were challenging. We realized that an upside down cup actually helps the hovercraft float, even though it reduces airflow to the fan, because it helps the fan maintain a high pressure environment under the craft. This is a video of us testing the hovercraft on water after the competition. It worked! We ended up placing 7th.

Wright Stuff SciOly 2019
This Science Olympiad event was absolutely nerve racking. The goal is to keep a rubber band powered glider in the air for as long as possible. I built a balsa wood glider with two wings, and designed it to constantly turn to the left so that it wouldn't hit a wall in the indoor testing room. We had two trials, and in the first trial I messed up the throw. As you can see in the video, the plane crashed right into the ground. My partner and I had three minutes to super glue the entire plane back together, and somehow the plane flew better than it ever had before. It was in the air for 110 seconds, something I'm really proud of! We were technically disqualified because we missed our competition time slot, but the judges let us fly our plane anyways. We might have had the best flight time at the event, but because we lost points from being disqualified we only got 11th out of ~ 80 teams.

FTC Autonomous code
I've loved helping build and design robots on my small FTC team over the last three years. Last year, I decided to build up the framework for a mimic auto. A mimic auto saves encoder values while a human is driving, and then plays those values back so that the robot can drive itself. From the saved encoder values we calculated the robot's change in x, y, and rotation in between each data point. The recorded x,y, and rotation then became setpoints for our control system. Using this method, we were able to get smooth curves into our auto, and we ended up having one of the highest scoring and most consistent autos in all of the San Diego region. In the video, our robot is the one on the left with the number 10092. This year we decided to start from scratch and pursue a global field tracking system, favoring drawing points in a program over recordings. This makes adjusting the auto path afterwards much easier.
